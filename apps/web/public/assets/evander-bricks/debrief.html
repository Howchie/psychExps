SONA Debrief Form

Project title: How many boxes can you open under pressure?

Supervisor: Dr Zachary Howard (CI)

Student Researcher:Mr Evander Agung (Honours)

1. What was I asked to do, and why was I asked to do it?

Today, you completed a Box Task that examined how well people perform and feel under time pressure at different levels of scheduling control. In this task, you earned coins by opening as many boxes as possible, moving at different speeds along conveyor belts. You could only open the boxes one at a time by clicking and holding each box. Some boxes were worth more than others, and the position in which these high-value boxes were presented and could be opened varied. You also completed a concurrent reaction-time task with a flashing red border and provided ratings of your experienced workload. We are interested in changes in your performance, objective and subjective workload across different time pressures and scheduling conditions, based on the speed of the conveyor belt and the position of the high-value box.

2. What is the psychological phenomenon this study tries to understand or explain?

This study aims to understand the effects of scheduling control and time pressure on mental workload and performance. Mental workload refers to the amount of mental effort and cognitive resources a person uses to complete a task and can be measured subjectively or objectively. Different factors, such as increased scheduling control and decreased time pressure, can help decrease individuals’ workload during tasks. However, we don’t yet fully understand whether a lack of control over task order (forced scheduling) significantly impacts workload and performance, and whether it differentially impacts your subjective experience of effort, versus your objective information-processing capacity and performance, beyond the effects of time pressure alone.

3. Why is it important this research is done?

In many modern work environments and everyday contexts, task order and scheduling are externally imposed. This may mean that even if we know which tasks are the most urgent and important, sometimes we may not be able to control or choose what to work on first. By understanding how forced, sub-optimal scheduling affects mental workload and performance, which may further lead to job dissatisfaction, burnout or reduced wellbeing, we can better design optimal workplace environments and develop everyday strategies that prioritise task scheduling control. In doing so, these may help reduce perceived subjective mental overload, while maintaining task performance, efficiency and mental well-being.

4. What is a proposed explanation for the psychological phenomenon that is being tested in this study? That is, what is the hypothesis?

The study tests three main hypotheses: (A) as time pressure increases, both subjective and objective workload will increase, and performance will decrease. (B) Subjective workload will be significantly more sensitive to the loss of scheduling control than objective workload and task performance. (C) Objective workload and task performance will remain relatively stable even when scheduling control decreases, beyond the effects of time pressure. To evaluate these hypotheses, we will conduct mixed effects regression analyses to determine the differential effects of scheduling control and time pressure on both subjective versus objective measures of workload and performance.

5. What are some critical design consideration that needed to be built into the study to enable testing this hypothesis?

We designed the Box Task such that participants experienced all levels of time pressure and scheduling control conditions, or a lack thereof, to control for individual differences in baseline cognitive capacity. To simulate the loss of scheduling control, the temporal position of the high value boxes was varied to create a controlled experience of opportunity cost and task-delay frustration, whilst randomising these positions to minimise practice effects. We also utilised two distinct methods to measure workload – a self-report scale for subjective effort and a response-time task for objective information-processing capacity. This allows us to observe whether these measures diverge when scheduling control is removed. Task performance was also standardised using a click-and-hold mechanism with 1000 ms target durations, providing an objective metric of performance accuracy. Trials were also self-paced to minimise fatigue, ensuring that the observed effects were due to the experimental manipulations.
